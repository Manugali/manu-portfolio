The land runs flat as a held breath. Sky so wide it makes everything below it feel small — including your loneliness, until it doesn't.

Lubbock was the first city I lived in when I came to America. I knew no one. I knew nothing. Campus, rent, a new accent in my own mouth. The kind of new that makes ordinary days feel like they are happening to someone you have not met yet.

Then — the friends.

Not a crowd. Never that. A few.

The kind who sit with you when the week is too long. Who know your face before you have learned how to wear it in a foreign place. Who stay up past reason because leaving the table would mean going back to the quiet. Who make a flat town in West Texas feel, for an hour, like somewhere you belong.

I did not know then that some people arrive as weather — temporary, you assume — and stay as climate. That you can meet someone in your twenties and still hear their voice clearly years later, across state lines, across the ordinary noise of a life that kept moving.

We drove nowhere special. Ate terrible food. Laughed too loud in cars that were too small. Survived semesters that felt endless. None of it photographed well. All of it mattered.

I left Lubbock in 2023. Iowa now. Different address. Different wind.

The friends did not stay in Texas. They came with me in the only way that counts — still here, still close, still picking up the phone, still the first people I want to tell something to. Distance turned out to be a rumor. Time, mostly polite.

When I say Lubbock, I do not always mean the flat land or the wide sky.

I mean them.

A handful of faces that turned a first American city from a place I survived into a place I loved. Not the whole city — just the rooms they were in. Just the hours that belonged to us.

That is what I think about when I think of Lubbock.

Not the geography.

The company.
